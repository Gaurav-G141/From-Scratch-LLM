This is a practical reading guide for a college-level musician fluent in Western notation. It assumes you already understand intervals, modes, chromaticism, meter, articulation, ornament, clefs, and transposition. The goal is not to re-teach those concepts, but to show where Byzantine notation is doing something structurally different.

Scope: modern/post-Early Byzantine neumatic notation, especially the Chrysanthine/post-1814 system, with occasional notes on older signs where the guide includes them.

## The Core Reframe

Byzantine notation is mostly intervallic and modal, not staff-positional.

The neumes tell you how the melody moves from the previous pitch. Martyriai, pitch letters, and phthorae tell you the current modal/pitch environment. Temporal and consonant signs modify duration, attack, vocal gesture, or phrasing.

So, do not read a Byzantine page as "where is this note on the staff?" Read it as:

1. What mode/pitch frame am I in?
2. What is my current pitch?
3. What interval does this neume or neume group produce from that pitch?
4. Does this sign alter duration, accent, ornament, scale, or ison behavior?
5. Has a phthora changed the modal system from here onward?

## Pitch Syllables

The guide presents modern pitch letters as a Byzantine equivalent to solfege, starting from `d`.

| Byzantine syllable | Reference pitch | Reading note |
|---|---:|---|
| Pa | D | Common reference/finalis for Mode I and hard chromatic Pa contexts |
| Vou | E | Second degree above Pa |
| Ga | F | Third degree above Pa |
| Di | G | Common center for Mode II/IV contexts |
| Ke | A | Fifth degree above Pa |
| Zo | B | Often altered, especially flattened in some modal contexts |
| Ni | C | Octave boundary/reference; important in Plagal IV contexts |

Treat these as modal pitch labels, not a guarantee of equal-tempered concert pitch. The modern theory described in the guide divides the octave into 72 commas.

## Combination Logic

The first big Western-reader trap is that stacked signs can be either cumulative or subordinated.

- Cumulative: the intervals add.
- Subordinated/hypotactic: one sign supplies the actual interval, while the other supplies accent/attack/quality. In such cases, the host sign's own upward second may not count.

| Combination | Result | How to think it |
|---|---:|---|
| Oligon over petasti | Up a third | Cumulative upward movement |
| Kentima over oligon | Up a fourth, unaccented | Kentima + oligon |
| Kentima over petasti | Up a fourth, accented | Same interval, petasti quality |
| Ypsili at right/middle of oligon | Up a fifth, unaccented | Subordinated; oligon is quality, not +second |
| Ypsili at right/middle of petasti | Up a fifth, accented | Subordinated; petasti supplies accent |
| Ypsili at left of oligon | Up a sixth, unaccented | Cumulative |
| Ypsili at left of petasti | Up a sixth, accented | Cumulative plus petasti accent |
| Ypsili next to kentima over oligon | Up a seventh, unaccented | Compound leap |
| Ypsili next to kentima over petasti | Up a seventh, accented | Compound leap with accent |
| Ypsili over kentima over oligon | Up an octave, unaccented | Large cumulative leap |
| Ypsili over kentima over petasti | Up an octave, accented | Large cumulative leap with accent |
| Two ypsili over oligon | Up a ninth, unaccented | Advanced/rare |
| Two ypsili over petasti | Up a ninth, accented | Advanced/rare |
| Elaphron over apostrophos | Down a fourth | Descending intervals add |
| Hamili over apostrophos | Down a sixth | Descending compound |
| Hamili over elaphron | Down a seventh | Descending compound |
| Hamili over elaphron over apostrophos | Down an octave | Source describes `-4 + -3 + -2` |
| Hamili over hamili | Down a ninth | Advanced/rare |
| Ison over petasti | Accented unison | Same pitch with accent |
| Apostrophos over oligon | Down a second, slightly accented | Oligon colors the descent |
| Apostrophos over petasti | Down a second, accented | Petasti colors the descent |
| Elaphron over petasti | Down a third, accented | Petasti colors the descent |
| Elaphron over apostrophos over petasti | Down a fourth, accented | Compound descent with accent |

## Two-Note Sequence Combinations

Some neume clusters are short sequences, not single interval signs.

| Combination | Result | Performance/reading point |
|---|---|---|
| Kentimata above oligon | Two ascending steps | Second note legato |
| Oligon above kentimata | Two ascending steps | First note legato |
| Oligon before kentimata | Same pitch result as oligon above kentimata | Often hints that an ascent will turn into a descent |
| Apostrophos under ison | Ison, then apostrophos | Repeat, then descend one step |
| Two stacked apostrophoi | Two descending steps | Same as two apostrophoi on one syllable |

## Time, Duration, and Rests

Default note value is one beat. In Western transcription this is often represented as a quarter note, but the guide notes that some repertory Westerners would notate mostly in eighths still uses one neume per note, yielding fast-looking tempi if mapped too literally.

| Symbol | Codepoint | Name | Effect |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 𝁿 | U+1D07F | Klasma ano | Add one beat |
| 𝃴 | U+1D0F4 | Klasma kato | Add one beat; usually equivalent to upper klasma except special petasti cases |
| 𝂏 | U+1D08F | Gorgon | Marked note and previous note share one beat |
| 𝃵 | U+1D0F5 | Lower gorgon | Same duration effect as gorgon; placement depends on host |
| 𝂕 | U+1D095 | Digorgon | Three-note group in one beat |
| 𝂖 | U+1D096 | Trigorgon | Four-note group in one beat |
| 𝂅 | U+1D085 | Apli | Add one beat |
| 𝂆 | U+1D086 | Dipli | Add two beats |
| 𝂇 | U+1D087 | Tripli | Add three beats |
| 𝂈 | U+1D088 | Tetrapli | Add four beats; rare |
| 𝂗 | U+1D097 | Argon | Specialized slowing, mainly over oligon + kentimata |
| 𝂘 | U+1D098 | Imidiargon | Like argon, with longer second note |
| 𝂙 | U+1D099 | Diargon | Like argon, with still longer second note |

Gorgon is the trap: it reaches backward. It is not simply "shorten the note under the sign." Digorgon and trigorgon involve a group around the marked note.

Punctuated gorgons imitate dotted rhythms, but the guide notes disagreement among authorities. The safe reading principle is: locate the quickened group and identify which member is lengthened.

| Sign type | Approximate result in the guide's logical scheme |
|---|---|
| Gorgon with dot before | Long-short triplet feel |
| Gorgon with dot after | Short-long triplet feel |
| Digorgon with dot before | First of three lengthened |
| Digorgon with dot after | Last of three lengthened |
| Dot above digorgon | Middle of three lengthened/accented |

| Symbol | Codepoint | Name | Effect |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 𝁾 | U+1D07E | Stavros | Breath mark |
| 𝂉 | U+1D089 | Koronis | Fermata |
| 𝂊 | U+1D08A | Leimma enos chronou | Quarter rest |
| 𝂋 | U+1D08B | Leimma dyo chronon | Half rest |

## Consonant Signs / Hypostases

These are vocal gesture, ornament, phrasing, and articulation signs. They are not consistently reducible to Western ornaments.

| Symbol | Codepoint | Name | Practical effect |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 𝁘 | U+1D058 | Varia | Accent/separation; also component of rests |
| 𝁛 | U+1D05B | Omalon | Light undulation between notes |
| 𝁜 | U+1D05C | Antikenoma | Lively ascent / grace-note-like gesture |
| 𝁚 | U+1D05A | Psifiston | Accent followed by diminuendo, often over a span |
| 𝁠 | U+1D060 | Eteron | Slur two notes, sometimes with slight undulation/grace behavior |
| 𝁻 | U+1D07B | Endofonon | Nasal tone; largely obsolete |
| 𝁼 | U+1D07C | Yfen kato | Tie/lengthener, no inserted grace note |
| 𝁽 | U+1D07D | Yfen ano | Upper tie/lengthener |

Eteron is not just a Western tie. The guide notes that if the second note tied is an ison, it is sung with slight upward undulation.

## Modes, Scales, and Commas

Modern theory in the guide divides the octave into 72 commas:

- Equal-tempered whole tone = 12 commas.
- Equal-tempered semitone = 6 commas.

Scale patterns:

| Scale type | Comma pattern | Main use |
|---|---|---|
| Diatonic | 10, 8, 12, 12, 10, 8, 12 | Modes I and IV, authentic and plagal |
| Soft chromatic | 8, 14, 8, 12, 8, 14, 8 | Often authentic Mode II |
| Hard chromatic ascending | 6, 20, 4, 12, 6, 20, 4 | Often Plagal II hard chromatic |
| Hard chromatic descending | 4, 20, 6, 12, 4, 20, 6 | Hard chromatic descent |
| Enharmonic, 1881 | 12, 12, 6, 12, 12, 12, 6 | Mode III and Varys |

Mode snapshot from the guide, with Western accidentals only approximate:

| Mode | Approximate guide description | Caveat |
|---|---|---|
| I | D-E-F-G-A-B-C-D; finalis D, sometimes A | Diatonic |
| Plagal I | A-B-C-D-E-F-G-A; finalis A fast, D moderate | B often flattened, especially with Agem |
| II | G-Ab-B-C-Db-E-F-G; finalis G, E, or D | Chromatic, typically soft |
| Plagal II | D-Eb-F#-G-A-Bb-C#-D | Chromatic, typically hard; soft form finalis G/Nenano, hard finalis D |
| III | F-G-A-B[flat]-C-D-E-F; finalis F | Enharmonic; usually needs phthorae/general sharp/flat |
| Varys | Enharmonic on F, or diatonic/proto-varys on B | Several variants |
| IV | G-A-B-C-D-E-F-G | Diatonic; Legetos behavior in moderate/fast settings |
| Plagal IV | C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C; finalis C | With Ni phthora, finalis F and B flattened |

The guide itself warns that presenting Byzantine modes as Western tonic-to-tonic scales is distorted. Tetrachords, finalis behavior, cadences, phthorae, and ison practice matter more than the staff-equivalent scale spelling.

## Western-Brain Traps

1. Vertical space is not staff pitch.
   - It matters for grouping and sign relationship, not absolute pitch height.
2. A "second" is modal, not necessarily ET whole/semitone behavior.
3. Oligon/petasti can be accent hosts rather than interval contributors.
4. Gorgon modifies a group and reaches backward.
5. Phthorae can change the scale/mode, not merely one pitch.
6. Default sharp/flat signs are not always fixed ET semitone accidentals.
7. Finalis is not simply tonic; modal cadences and tetrachord/ison behavior matter.
8. Ison is not bass harmony or root motion.
9. Meter may be implied, flexible, or pedagogical rather than grid-defining.
10. Some older signs are genuinely uncertain in the source; do not over-interpret rare hypostases.

## If You Want to Read a Basic Score

1. Identify initial martyria and pitch syllable.
2. Set your pitch cursor.
3. Move by interval signs, using the combination table for stacked signs.
4. Keep a separate duration layer for klasma/apli/gorgon signs.
5. Watch for phthorae; if one appears, update the scale frame.
6. Treat ison separately from melody.
7. Use medial martyriai as checkpoints. If your pitch cursor disagrees with them, you probably missed a combination, phthora, or octave/register assumption.
